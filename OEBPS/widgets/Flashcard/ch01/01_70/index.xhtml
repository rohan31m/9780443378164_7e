Eyeball: Horizontal Section

Comment

The eyeball has three layers: an external fibrous layer consisting of the sclera and transparent cornea; a middle vascular pigmented layer consisting of the choroid, ciliary body, and iris; and an internal neural layer, the retina.

The fovea centralis, a central depression in the macula, is an avascular region that contains cones but no rods. This area provides the most acute vision.

Light passes to the retina through the refractive media of the eye, which consists of the cornea, aqueous humor, lens, and vitreous humor.

Clinical

An opacity of the lens is called a cataract. Treatment often involves surgically removing the lens, implanting a plastic lens, and correcting the vision with glasses. Glaucoma is an increase in ocular pressure, usually due to poor reabsorption of aqueous humor. This increased pressure may damage the retina.

Common vision disorders include: (1) Myopia, or nearsightedness; (2) Hyperopia, or farsightedness; (3) Astigmatism, a nonspherical cornea; and (4) Presbyopia, an age-related loss of accommodative ability (the lens is less flexible).
