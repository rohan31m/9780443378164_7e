Scapulohumeral Dissection

Origin (proximal): The subscapularis rises from the medial two-thirds of the subscapular fossa and from the lower two-thirds of the lateral border of the scapula.

Insertion (distal): The fibers converge in a tendon that is inserted into the lesser tubercle of the humerus and the anterior portion of the shoulder joint capsule.

Action: As a rotator cuff muscle, the subscapularis helps stabilize the shoulder joint and prevents anterior displacement of the humerus. It also medially rotates and adducts the humerus (arm) at the shoulder.

Innervation: Upper and lower subscapular nerves (C5 and C6).

Comment: The tendon of the muscle is separated from the neck of the scapula by the subscapular bursa.

Along with the supraspinatus, infraspinatus, and teres minor muscles, the subscapularis is the fourth muscle of the rotator cuff group (called the “SITS” muscles).

The subscapularis and serratus anterior muscles together form a functional (physiological) articulation of the shoulder called the scapulothoracic joint. The joint is between the muscles, the scapula, and the loose connective tissue trapped between these two muscles, which permits a gliding movement of the scapula on the chest wall. Scapular movements at this joint include elevation, depression, protraction, retraction, and rotation.

Clinical: Weakness of the subscapularis alone is difficult to isolate, as it is one of the four rotator cuff muscles (SITS muscles) that stabilize the shoulder joint.
