Shoulder (Glenohumeral) Joint: Anterior View

Comment: The shoulder is a multiaxial synovial ball-and-socket (spheroidal) joint. Movements include abduction and adduction, flexion and extension, and rotation and circumduction. The shallow glenoid cavity of the scapula permits extensive movement at the shoulder but also makes this joint vulnerable to dislocation. The four tendons of the rotator cuff muscles help stabilize the joint.

Also shown is the acromioclavicular joint, a synovial plane joint between the acromion and clavicle. This joint permits gliding movement as the arm is raised and the scapula rotates.

Although the clavicle is the first long bone to ossify (by intramembranous ossification), it is the last long bone to fuse its epiphyses (25-30 years)!

Clinical: Because of the wide range of motion at the shoulder and its rather shallow glenoid cavity, this joint is one of the most frequently dislocated joints in the body. This glenohumeral joint usually dislocates in an anterior or anteroinferior direction (subcoracoid dislocation) and can place the axillary and musculocutaneous nerves of the brachial plexus at risk for injury.

The axillary nerve is the most frequently injured nerve in a traction-type injury, and the musculocutaneous nerve may also be injured during glenohumeral joint dislocations.
