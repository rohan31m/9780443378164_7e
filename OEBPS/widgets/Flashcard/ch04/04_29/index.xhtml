Schematic Cross Section of Abdomen at T12

Comment

In this cross section of the upper abdomen at the T12 vertebral level, the intraperitoneal viscera are represented by the liver, stomach, and spleen. Retroperitoneal viscera lying beneath the parietal peritoneum in the posterior abdominal wall include the right and left kidneys, suprarenal (adrenal) glands, aorta, and inferior vena cava.

Within the hepatoduodenal portion of the lesser omentum, one can see the portal vein, common bile duct, and proper hepatic artery.

The parietal peritoneum lines the inner aspect of the abdominal wall and is innervated by the somatic afferent fibers of spinal nerves (pain is well localized). The visceral peritoneum, on the other hand, is innervated by visceral afferents of the autonomic nervous system and pain is more poorly localized, giving rise to referred pain.

Clinical

Note that the kidneys and adrenal glands are retroperitoneal organs, which lie posterior to the parietal peritoneum covering the posterior abdominal wall. Therefore, in some instances, these organs may be accessed surgically without entering the peritoneal cavity, thus reducing the chance for intraabdominal infection. The fat surrounding the kidneys is important in maintaining their proper position within the posterior abdominal wall.
