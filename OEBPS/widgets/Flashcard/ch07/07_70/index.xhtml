Superficial Nerves and Veins of Lower Limb: Posterior View

Comment: The sural nerve is formed by the union of a cutaneous nerve from the tibial nerve and a small communicating branch from the common fibular nerve. It courses with the small saphenous vein.

The small saphenous vein receives numerous superficial tributaries. Perforating branches from the small saphenous vein and its tributaries communicate with deep veins accompanying the tibial artery and its branches. The small saphenous vein drains into the popliteal vein behind the knee.

The superficial and deep veins of the lower limb, similar to the veins of the upper limb, possess venous valves to aid in venous return to the heart against gravity.

Superficial lymphatics accompany the saphenous veins and their tributaries and collect in the popliteal nodes (knee) and superficial inguinal nodes (in the saphenous opening). Deep lymphatics accompany the deep veins and ultimately collect in the deep inguinal nodes and then into the internal iliac nodes of the pelvis.

Clinical: Venous stasis can have grave consequences for the veins of the lower limb. Deep venous thrombosis (DVT) can result and a thromboembolus can pass toward the heart and become lodged in the small capillary networks of the lung, obstructing a pulmonary artery. Clinical risk factors include postsurgical immobility, muscular inactivity, vessel trauma, infection, paralysis, malignancy, and pregnancy.
