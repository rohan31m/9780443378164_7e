Pericardial Sac

Comment: The pericardial cavity is a potential space between the visceral serous pericardium (epicardium) and the parietal serous pericardium, which lines the inner surface of the fibrous pericardium. The visceral serous pericardium reflects off the heart around these large vessels and becomes the parietal serous pericardial layer. The heart (removed in this illustration) lies within the pericardial sac.

The oblique pericardial sinus, a cul-de-sac behind the left atrium of the heart, is another reflection of serous pericardium surrounding the pulmonary veins.

Pain sensation from the fibrous pericardium is conveyed via the paired phrenic nerves (C3-C5), which course through this layer on their way to innervate the abdominal diaphragm.

Clinical: The transverse sinus (#2) is a space between the reflections of the serous pericardium, which is posterior to the ascending aorta and pulmonary trunk, and anterior to the superior vena cava. This is a clinically important area because clamping these vessels can completely stop ventricular outflow.
